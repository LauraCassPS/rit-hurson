There is a way to divorce without going to court

Most people assume divorce means solicitors' letters going back and forth, and a courtroom at the end of it. There is another route, and for many families it is the better one.

What collaborative divorce is

Collaborative divorce is a legal process where both people stay out of court. Each of you has your own collaboratively trained solicitor, and the two of you work through everything, the finances, the arrangements for the children, the future, in a series of meetings rather than through litigation.

Where it helps, other professionals join the team: a financial specialist, a pension expert, or a family consultant like Rita. Everyone signs an agreement at the start to resolve things out of court. That single commitment changes the tone of everything that follows.

Where Rita fits

On a collaborative team, Rita is the family consultant. Her focus is you: helping you prepare for the meetings, make sense of the advice, and communicate through the hardest parts, including the conversations about the children.

The solicitors handle the law. The financial people handle the numbers. Rita holds the human side, and keeps you steady enough to make good decisions. Where collaborative suits your situation she will guide you toward it, and where a case cannot settle this way, she stays with you through whatever route it takes.

Divorce brings a lot at once, and none of it comes with instructions

There's the legal process itself, with its own timelines, terminology and procedure.

There's the financial picture to untangle, sometimes decades of shared assets, income and outgoings.

If there are children, there are decisions that will shape their lives for years.

And underneath all of it, the emotional weight of the relationship ending, which doesn't wait for the practical side to be dealt with first.

Most people find themselves managing all of this simultaneously, for the first time, while also trying to keep working, keep parenting, and keep functioning. Solicitors advise on the law. Accountants advise on the numbers. Making sense of the whole picture, while you work through it, is where Rita comes in.

You know how to handle things. This is just unfamiliar territory.

You're used to managing complexity, work, family, a full life. Separation and divorce bring a different kind of complexity: legal processes you've never had reason to learn, financial decisions with long-term consequences, and a lot of advice coming at you from different directions, often in language that assumes you already know what it means.

You don't need it simplified because you can't follow it. You need it explained properly, what your solicitor or accountant is telling you, translated into a plan you can act on, with the decisions still yours to make.

From managing chaos to making decisions

Working with Rita, clients move from reacting to whatever comes next, to actually directing the process. Meetings with solicitors stop feeling like something to get through, and start feeling like conversations you're prepared for. The financial picture stops being overwhelming and becomes something you can see clearly and plan around. And because the process is calmer and better managed, there's less strain on the relationships that have to continue afterwards, particularly where children are involved.

Most clients describe it the same way. They stop second-guessing themselves, they're no longer carrying it alone, and they trust that the decisions being made are the right ones for them and their family.
